

\subsection{Results in voting}

We next examine how exposure to refugee camps affected electoral participation and support for left-wing parties. \Cref{table:all_parties_combined} reports estimates for voter turnout, the socialist party (SFIO: “Section Fran\c{c}aise de l’Internationale Ouvri`ere”), and the communist party (PCF: “Parti Communiste Français"). For each outcome, the first specification compares municipalities located within 1 km of a camp to those between 20 and 40 km, while the second specification implements \Cref{equation:political} and allows for spatial spillovers by including concentric distance rings up to 30 km. The results show that proximity to refugee camps increased electoral participation. Municipalities located within 1 km of a camp experienced a rise in turnout of about 2.0–2.6 percentage points relative to the control group, which corresponds to an increase of around 2–3\% relative to the baseline level of participation. The spatial specification indicates that this effect is concentrated very close to the camps and declines rapidly with distance.

Turning to party support, the estimates reveal a redistribution of votes within the left. Municipalities located within 1 km of a camp show a statistically significant decline in the socialist vote share of around 1.5–2.8 percentage points, which represents a reduction of 8-15\% compared to the pretreatment mean. Meanwhile, the communist vote share increases by approximately 1.7–2.4 percentage points; an increase of approximately 30-40\% of the communist vote share. The spatial specification shows that the increase in communist support extends beyond the immediate vicinity of the camps, remaining positive and statistically significant up to roughly 25 km, whereas the reduction in socialist support is concentrated closer to the camps. Right-wing parties are not comparable before and after the shock because there is no single party that exists continuously throughout the entire period of analysis.\footnote{Throughout the interwar and post-war years, the composition of the French right changed substantially. Key parties present before the war—such as the Fédération républicaine or the Alliance démocratique—either disappeared, were absorbed, or were replaced after 1945 by entirely new formations like the MRP or, later, the Gaullist RPF.} We report the results for right-wing support in the Appendix using the party aggregation proposed by \citet{piketty_histoire_2023}, which yields a positive effect that is only statistically significant at the 10\% level.

\Cref{fig:turnout_event_study} presents the results of the dynamic DiD analysis, estimating the effect of proximity to refugee camps on voter turnout across distance rings. The figure shows a clear increase in turnout in municipalities located within 1 km of a camp, with effects that gradually decline over time until 1956. Positive but smaller effects are also observed for municipalities located 1–5 km and 5–10 km from the camps. In contrast, there is no statistically significant effect for municipalities located between 15 and 20 km.  \Cref{fig:event_study_all} presents the dynamic DiD estimates for both the communist and the socialist parties. The French left underwent important political realignments during the pre-treatment period, notably the Cartel des Gauches in 1924 and the Popular Front alliance in 1936, which likely explain the observed pre-trends, particularly around the 1924 election. After the war, the estimates show a clear break in the trend: support for the communist party increases sharply in 1945, while the socialist party experiences a decline, with both effects gradually fading over time. \Cref{fig:PCF_all} and \Cref{fig:SFIO_all} in the Appendix show that these effects attenuate with distance from the camps. While the magnitude of the estimates decreases for municipalities located further away, the presence of spillovers supports the use of municipalities located between 20 and 40 km from the camps as the control group.

We next explore whether the political effects we document depend on the size of the refugee camps to which local populations were exposed. \Cref{fig:effect_size} reports the estimated effects on the vote shares of the socialist and communist parties for municipalities located within 1 km of a camp, distinguishing camps by size. Camp size is defined according to quartiles of the refugee population hosted in each camp (see \Cref{fig:bars_camps_sizes} in the Appendix for the definition of these quartiles). The magnitude of the effects generally grows with camp size, reaching its largest values for medium and large camps. In contrast, the effects are smaller for municipalities exposed to very small camps and are close to zero for the group with missing size information. Overall, the results suggest that the political impact of refugee camps is stronger where the local population was exposed to a larger concentration of refugees, consistent with a more intense local exposure generating larger electoral responses.

\subsection{Results in mobilization}


We now examine whether the effects differ across the political spectrum to explore political polarization and diffusion of ideas. We explore the relationship between the location of the camps and the resistance movement to explore the effect on the behaviour among a politically selective minority rather than population-wide responses. These measures reflect actions taken by individuals who were already highly engaged in political or civic life. Several organizations were especially active in mobilizing around the arrival of the Spanish refugees: the General Confederation of Labour (CGT), professional unions and cooperative federations. Members of these groups were more likely to interact directly with the Republican refugees. If ideas brought by the refugees diffused locally, politically active individuals are the ones most likely to internalize and act upon them. Any diffusion mechanism would therefore be expected to appear at the political extremes, where responses may diverge from those of the average voter.


\Cref{fig:resistants} displays coefficient estimates by distance to the nearest refugee camp, with each point corresponding to a separate regression. The results indicate a positive association between proximity to a camp and resistance participation. This effect is concentrated within 3 km and disappears at larger distances. Within this radius, camp proximity is associated with an approximately 13\% increase in the expected number of individuals participating in the resistance, corresponding to about 1.5 additional individuals per municipality. This pattern is consistent with the results on the communist party, considering the links between the PCF and the resistance movement.

We further explore the political mobilization impact of exposure to Spanish refugees by examining its effect on the creation of associations. To assess this, we classify associations based on their names and descriptions of activities as listed in the Registre National des Associations. Our main results regarding these outcomes are presented in \autoref{tab:att_associations_large_medium}\footnote{The corresponding event study plots are available in \autoref{apdx:}}. Specifically, we identify four types of associations of interest: associations related to Migrants and Refugees, Solidarity associations, Left-wing and Union associations, and those focused on military and Resistance activities\footnote{We determine whether an association belongs to one of these categories based on a list of key words that unequivocally indicate the type of activity. These lists of key words are available in the Appendix.}. We then apply the same empirical strategies as before at the canton level, comparing localities that have welcomed large and medium camps of refugees with those that have not. Our findings show a positive effect on the creation of associations after the Retirada for associations related to refugee support, as well as for unions and left-wing groups. As a placebo, we also demonstrate that other types of associations, such as sports and religious groups, do not exhibit the same effects (see columns (5) and (6), showing results for these types of associations). While this effect aligns with the political mobilization observed through voting patterns post-WWII, as well as the backlash against the Socialist Party and the Radicals, our results also provide evidence of the type of interactions that may have contributed to the rise in both Resistance behaviour and the transmission of values.
